# Handprint Product Brief

## One-Sentence Description

Handprint helps people discover useful civic and community action within reach, participate with verified World Enablers, and grow a personal visual identity from real-world contribution.

## Core Thesis

People are tired of outrage and abstraction. They want to feel useful, connected, and proud of tangible local impact. Handprint converts local action into visible civic identity.

## Product Category

Local civic identity engine.

Handprint is adjacent to event discovery, volunteering, community organizing, and social networking, but it should not launch as a generic version of any of them.

## Primary User

A person who wants to do something useful locally but does not know where to start.

Early adopter traits:

- Values community involvement.
- Wants low-friction entry points.
- Is motivated by visible progress.
- May be civic-minded without being politically obsessive.
- Wants agency without doom.

## Primary World Enabler

A trusted local individual or organization that needs participants for useful community action.

Examples:

- Nonprofits
- Campus organizations
- Mutual aid groups
- Churches and faith communities
- Neighborhood associations
- Local civic forums
- Schools and clubs after legal review

## Product Promise

"Here are meaningful things within reach, and here is the visible mark your participation leaves."

For World Enablers:

"Here are people ready to help, and here is a verified record of what your organization mobilized, delivered, and stewarded."

## Differentiation

Most platforms reward posting, attention, or ideological performance. Handprint rewards verified participation.

Most ad platforms sell attention. Handprint should sell carefully governed reach into real action, transparent fundraising, and sponsor-backed capacity without selling trust.

## First Launch Positioning

Start as a focused pilot for one dense local community:

> Find useful local action this week. Join it. Watch your Handprint grow.

## Strategic Risks

- Cold start: not enough local activity.
- Neutrality drift: perceived ideological capture.
- Trust and safety: harmful or misleading events.
- Incumbent gravity: users already rely on Facebook, Eventbrite, Meetup, and group chats.
- Weak identity loop: if the handprint is not emotionally satisfying, the app becomes a directory.
